Australia Recruits Doctors that Ireland Struggles to Retain

By Kelly Waldron

February 19, 2023

Emigration has long formed a part of the Irish story. From the Irish famine to the 2008 recession, Ireland has experienced waves of citizens leaving the country and formed an extensive diaspora abroad, Australia being no exception.

While Ireland in recent years has maintained a positive net migration rate, there are still significant factors pushing people to emigrate, especially the younger generation, namely the cost of living.

One group leaving in greater numbers are doctors. Australia, while far, appears to be an obvious choice for Irish medical graduates, as their medical degrees are automatically recognized allowing them to practice there and the working conditions are better.

Ireland’s public healthcare system, the HSE, has long been plagued with staffing shortages and underfunding–demanding more hours of employees in strenuous conditions.

The number of Irish doctors who are granted visas allowing them to work in Australia has continued to increase over the last two decades.

The vast majority of those who are granted these visas are between the ages of 20 and 29, meaning they are leaving soon if not directly after obtaining their degree.

Between June 2021 and 2022, 405 visas were granted to Irish medical doctors by the Australian government, most of whom are under the age of 30. That is equivalent to almost half the number of people, 888 graduates, who became registered doctors from medical schools in Ireland last year.

The British Medical Journal recently reported that 62 of 77 medical students who graduated in 2021 from University College Cork are currently practicing in Australia.

The shortage of practicing doctors in Ireland is not a problem of training. In fact, Ireland boasts the highest number of people graduating from medical schools than any other country in the OECD, a group of 38 countries.

The recognition of their qualifications, as well as shared language make these candidates ideal recruits for the Australian healthcare system, which is heavily reliant on an influx of skilled labor from abroad.

The Victorian government has jumped on this opportunity and paid for billboard advertising healthcare jobs, strategically placed near hospitals in Dublin.

Others have joked about the idea of Ireland doing the same thing and placing billboards advertising healthcare jobs in Australia. Bar the low risk of sunburn, the prospect seems less appealing.

Despite other provinces having relocation incentives for incoming medical practitioners, Western Australia has become the destination of choice for doctors from Ireland.
